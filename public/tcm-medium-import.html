The Tongue Does Not Lie

Instead, he asked to see my tongue. Then he took my pulse — not the usual 15 seconds, but three full minutes on each wrist. When he finished, he said something that stopped me cold: "Your Spleen Qi is depleted. You've been eating the wrong foods for your body type."

I had never heard of "Spleen Qi." I didn't know what "body constitution" meant. I was a 34-year-old tech worker who tracked everything — sleep score, HRV, steps, macros — and I had completely missed an entire dimension of health that Chinese medicine had been mapping for millennia.

So I made a deal with myself: 30 days. No PPIs. No Western treatments unless absolutely necessary. Just TCM — herbal formulas, dietary changes based on my constitution, acupressure, and seasonal eating. I documented everything.

I tested as Qi Deficiency with secondary Damp-Heat. Translation: my energy was running on empty, and my digestion was inflamed. The fix wasn't a pill. It was a diet change so specific it felt like a custom prescription.

What I Actually Ate for 30 Days

The practitioner pointed to a thick white coating in the center of my tongue. "Dampness," he said. "Your digestion is waterlogged."

His prescription: No cold foods or raw vegetables for a week. No smoothies. No salads. No iced water.

Morning: Warm millet congee with ginger and goji berries
Lunch: Steamed fish with bitter greens, barley tea
Dinner: Bone broth soup with root vegetables and mushrooms
Snacks: Roasted chestnuts, dried jujubes, warm herbal tea

By day 5, my bloating had dropped by about 70%. My digestion became regular for the first time in years. The white coating on my tongue started to fade.

Eating With the Seasons

TCM doesn't just personalize for your body type. It adjusts for the time of year. Late spring and early summer fall under the Wood and Fire elements — the season of the Liver and Heart. The recommendation: light, cooling foods. Mung beans, cucumber, bitter greens. Less meat. Bed by 11 PM. Wake with the sun.

I had never eaten with the seasons before. I used to eat avocados and salads in winter because that's what "healthy" meant. TCM says winter is for slow-cooked stews and warming foods. Summer is for light, cooling meals. When I aligned my eating with the calendar, my afternoon energy crashes disappeared.

The Real Surprise

Here's what caught me off guard: the herbs and acupuncture weren't the game-changers. The lifestyle shifts were. Going to bed earlier. Eating warm, cooked food. Chewing thoroughly. Not drinking ice water with meals.
